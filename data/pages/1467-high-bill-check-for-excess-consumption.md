# High Bill? Check for Excess Consumption

Please note the [My360 Portal](/1646/Register-for-My360-Portal) is now open for registration. Use this portal to monitor your water usage and setup alerts so you can proactively monitor your water usage to prevent a high bill from occurring.

**If you are unable to find and repair leaks by following the steps below or are uncomfortable operating shut-off valves, you may need to contact a professional plumber. Township Staff cannot perform any plumbing repairs on privately owned piping.**

High bills usually mean excess consumption and potentially hundreds of gallons of water being used. This could cost as much as several hundred dollars per month and thousands of dollars per quarter. Some examples of excess consumption include:

* Dripping Faucet: Consumes 15 gallons a day, or 60 cubic feet per month.
* Running Toilet: May consume up to 12 gallons per minute. Toilet leaks are the most common cause of high bill issues.
* Underground Sprinkler System Issues: Breaks in underground sprinkler pipes or damaged sprinkler heads consume large amounts of water. Improperly timed sprinklers water excessively and waste water while increasing bills.
* Sump Pump with Water-operated Backup: Sump Pumps with water-operated backups can become stuck in the backup mode after a power outage or develop a leak that will be discharged via the sump pump and not result in an obvious accumulation of water on the floor. A sump pump running excessively is the sign of a problem.

Please note that the excess water use, identification, and repair of problems within your private plumbing system, as well as the associated costs, are the responsibility of the property owner.

## Meter Measurement, Leaks & Age

Water meters are simple mechanical devices that register water usage via a spinning disc inside the meter called a measuring element. The measuring element can register water usage when water is actively being used at a property. As the meter ages, the measuring element can gradually slow down over time and will eventually slightly under register water in favor of the customer, or the measuring element can freeze and stop registering water entirely. Due to this gradual slow down of the measuring element, the industry standard is to target meters 10 years or older for replacement. A "high reading" on an account only occurs when there is actual high usage at a property due to some of the issues listed later on this page or because the account is not getting reads and the billing system is estimating the account. Occasionally, these estimated reads can result in an overestimated reading.

Meters can develop leaks in two places, at the base of the meter or at the coupling. The meter base plate can fail completely if the water inside the meter freezes and cracks as a result of not being properly heated in the winter time. The meter base plate can also develop pinholes as they get older. In the case of meter leaks at the base of the water meter, because the water is leaking out of the bottom and is not passing through the measuring element, it is not being registered properly on the meter. As such, adjustments are not issued for leaking meter issues. Coupling leaks are typically small drips at the coupling that result from a gradual loosening of the coupling, and they just need to be tightened. The usage from such drips is negligible. As a result, adjustments are not issued for leaking meter issues.

All of the Township water meters measure in cubic feet, not gallons, and 1 cubic feet is equal to 7.48 gallons.  All usage on the utility bills is shown as "units" where 1 unit = 100 cubic feet = 748 gallons.  A typical single family home without lawn sprinkling uses between 20 and 40 units in a quarter depending on the number of bathrooms and number of people in the house.

## Estimated Reads & Adjusted Bills

Water meter readings are obtained via radio signal using a radio remote device connected to the meter. The Township does not manually read individual meetings. Occasionally, this radio remote can have a weak signal or stop transmitting entirely. Meters can also occasionally become frozen and the spinning disc inside stops moving. An account with a non-transmitting radio remote or a frozen meter will result in an estimated bill. The estimate will be indicated on your bill with a large **E**printed next to the reading. If your account is being estimated, you will receive a letter requesting meter service. It is possible when an account is estimated for several quarters in a row that when the meter is serviced and an actual reading is taken, the total usage on the account will either be under or over estimated. In the case that the account is being underestimated, an additional charge will be applied to the account. In the case the reading is overestimated, a credit will be applied to the account. If you are utilizing the [customer web portal My360](/1646/Register-for-My360-Portal) and consistently see zero usage or no readings at all or see an **E**on your bill, please contact us so we can schedule an appointment to resolve your estimated bill.

## Meter Testing

If a customer still feels there is an issue with the water meter, the Township does have a meter testing process. At the request of a customer, the water meter will be removed from the property and sent to a third party testing company to see if it meets the standards of the American Water Works Association (AWWA), which the NJ Board of Public Utilities uses as a guide for meter testing. If the meter test shows that the meter meets the AWWA standards, a $150 fee will be applied to the customer's account. If the meter comes back as not meeting the AWWA standards, the Township will absorb the cost of the meter test.

## Step-by-Step Instructions for Checking for Excess Water Consumption

### Step 1: Check the Consumption Indicator on the water meter

* Your water meter is a Neptune meter and is inside the house typically located in the basement, crawl space, utility room, or garage. In very rare circumstances it may be located in a manhole in the yard, but this is extremely uncommon.
* Ensure no water is actively being used in the house and check the consumption indicator on your water meter. It should be completely still if there is no water being used. Below are some of the possible appearances for the consumption indicator:
  + If your meter looks like this, the **red triangle**is the consumption indicator. If it is moving continuously, something at the property is continuously consuming water. Proceed to Step 2.
  + If your meter looks like this, the **red clock dial** is the consumption indicator. If it is moving continuously, something at the property is continuously consuming water. Proceed to Step 2.
  + If your meter looks like this, shine a bright flashlight for 15 to 30 second to trigger the display. A faucet image is the consumption indicator. If it is flashing you have intermittent consumption. If it is solid, something at the property is continuously consuming water. Proceed to Step 2.
  + If your meter looks like this, then a faucet image is the consumption indicator. If the faucet is shown, something at the property is continuously consuming water. Proceed to Step 2.

### Step 2: Checking for private plumbing issues (other than toilets)

If your Consumption Indicator on the meter is active, the next step is to check for private plumbing issues. If the problem is related to a toilet, go to Step 3 for more information. Depending on your level of comfort with plumbing and shut-off valves, you may want to call a professional plumber to assist you.

* Check all water-using appliances and fixtures one-by-one, checking the consumption indicator as you go. Turn-off valves as appropriate to isolate the problem. Appliances and fixtures include washing machines, dishwashers, ice makers, humidifiers, boilers, irrigation systems, outside hose bibs, water softeners, showers, faucets, and especially toilets.
* Some Sump Pumps have a water-powered back-up that can be triggered during a power failure and use excessive water.
* Continue until you have isolated the faulty device(s).
* Make your repairs or have a professional plumber make the repairs.
* Check the consumption indicator on the meter once more to be sure the problem has been resolved.

### Step 3: Checking your Toilet(s)

The most common leak in a household is caused by a faulty toilet(s). There are three ways to check for toilet leaks.

1. **Check the bowl for ripples.**

+ Flush the toilet and wait five (5) minutes.
+ Check the water in the bowl for ripples.

1. **Perform a dye test.**

+ Simply put a few drops of food coloring in the tank or pick up a free dye tab from the Water Department at Town Hall.
+ After an hour, check if colored water is in the bowl.

If you see ripples in the bowl or if colored water is in the bowl, you likely have an “improper seating leak.”

This problem can be corrected, depending on the cause, by:

+ Tightening a loose handle
+ Straightening the control arm if it is rubbing
+ Replacing a sticking rod guide or ball rod
+ Cleaning a corroded valve seat
+ Replacing a faulty tank ball

1. **Check the tank.**

+ Remove the top of the tank.
+ Find the overflow tube in the center of the tank.
+ Check the water level. It should be about one (1) inch below the top of the overflow.

If the water is above the overflow, you likely have a “continuous overflow leak."

This problem can be corrected, depending on the cause, by:

+ Bending the end of the float ball rod down
+ Replacing a float ball which has filled with water
+ Replacing a faulty or corroded float ball shut-off valve
