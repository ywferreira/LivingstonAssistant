# Discolored Water, Reduced Pressure & Other Quality Issues

## Discolored Water

Discolored water may be due to hydrant flushing, a water main break, valve exercising, nearby construction, or other use of a fire hydrant such as fire response, road construction, or street sweeping. When pipelines in the streets are disturbed, mineral sediments may sometimes break loose and cause rust or dirty-looking water. The sediments are mineral deposits that naturally occur in water.

To clear the discoloration, run the cold water tap for 30 to 60 minutes to clear the pipes. Flush the internal cold water plumbing lines until all faucets run clear. Start at the lowest level of your home and work your way up to the highest level. (NOTE: **Never** run the hot water until the cold water looks clear. Running hot water can draw discolored water into your hot water heater, which could cause long-term damage to your system.)

## Reduced Pressure

The pressure at a particular location in Livingston is essentially static and is based on the elevation, proximity to a water tank, and proximity to a system pump. Depending on the tank levels and the system demand, the pressure could vary between 5 and 10 psi, but the baseline pressure at a property is fixed and cannot be decreased or increased. The pressure within the Township varies from 40 psi to 160 psi depending on the location. If your street side pressure is over 80 psi, the plumbing code requires that a pressure reducing valve (PRV) be installed to reduce the incoming pressure. There is also a seasonal pressure drop in the early morning from mid-May to mid-September due to so many sprinkler systems running as well. Please note that Livingston has a three-day a week [lawn sprinkler ordinance](https://www.livingstonnj.org/464/Watering-Your-Lawn) and that it is not necessary to irrigate lawns daily.

The loose sediment from hydrant flushing, water main breaks, and construction can cause discolored water which can potentially clog and cause a reduced pressure in some plumbing equipment such as:

* Whole house filters
* Water softeners
* PRV
* Aerators on faucets
* Water meters

Beside clogged plumbing equipment, reduced pressure can also be caused by internal plumbing issues such as:

* A failed PRV
* A collapsed or broken internal shut off valve
* Excessive water use at the property. See [High Bill? Check for Leaks](/1467/High-Bill-Check-for-Excess-Consumption) for more information
* Frozen pipes
* Excessive calcium deposits on faucet aerators and shower heads
* Broken water service line in front yard. The water service line past the shutoff is the property owner's responsibility to dig and repair. See [Utility Service Line Ownership](/1568/Utility-Service-Line-Ownership) for more information.

## Air in Water

Similar to discolored water, if you experience sporadic or a pulsating stream of white, or "milky", water from your fixtures when you first use them, it is a result of air. This is usually caused by a small amount of air trapped in your plumbing lines and will resolve itself through normal water usage. This trapped air is caused by hydrant flushing, a water main break, valve exercising, nearby construction, or other use of a fire hydrant such as fire response, road construction, or street sweeping.

## Hard Water

Hardness in water is a measurement of the mineral content in the water, primarily calcium and magnesium. The well water sources in Livingston are considered above average in hardness. Hardness in water is a secondary or aesthetic criteria, not a health-based criteria. Hard water will result in mineral deposits in cookware, dishware, aerator fixtures, glassware, and other appliances and plumbing elements. As calcium deposits form overtime, they can require preventive cleaning to descale the buildup with vinegar or other cleaning products such as calcium/lime removers. There are also specific detergents that are specially formulated for hard water that can be used in the dishwasher and clothes washer. Some consumers may elect to install a water softener at the property. This is up to individual consumer preference. When setting up the softener, it is generally recommended to use a setting of 14 grains per gallon or GPG (240 mg/L or ppm) for the softener and adjust up or down accordingly based on how hard or soft the consumer would like the water to be. Typically, the primary drinking water supply tap is not softened as many softening systems add sodium to the water.

## Taste & Odor

The Township is required to add chlorine to the water by state and federal standards. The chlorine level in the water varies depending on the proximity of a property to a well house, bulk purchase point, tank, or pumping station. There may be some taste and odor concerns due to this required chlorine.

Another thing that can cause a change in the taste of water is the change in seasons. The eastern edge of Livingston along the West Orange border is primarily supplied by the purchase of surface water from New Jersey American Water. During sprinkler season, this area is supplemented with Livingston well water. In the spring, as the amount of supplemented well water increases, and in the fall, as the amount of supplemented well water decreases, changes in taste and odor could be perceived. The purchased surface water from New Jersey American Water is from reservoirs which have seasonal temperature changes as well as the potential for algae blooms. The reservoir algal activity generally occurs every year, particularly in the summer. While the water is treated to meet health-based drinking water standards, algae species may still impart an earthy and/or musty taste to drinking water. This presents negative aesthetic effects on drinking water via taste and odor. In the case that algae blooms have a taste and odor impact on the water, it will generally lessen as the weather cools.

Point of use treatment systems such as water softeners, whole house filters, or refrigerator filters need to be maintained and changed according to manufacturer recommendations. A filter past its useful life can start bio-foul and cause significant taste and odor issues. Also, if a consumer has recently changed medications, supplements, or diet, they may perceive a change in taste in the water.

A sewer gas smell is typically not related to the water. Certain fixtures at a property may produce an odor when the water is first turned on after not being used for a while. This can be the result of sewer gases trapped in the p-trap under the sink being released or if the sink has overflow drains. Pouring a small amount of bleach or vinegar down the drain periodically can help reduce these odors.

Finally, if a consumer previously lived elsewhere, the source water at the prior residence may have had different hardness and mineral content, so the consumer may perceive a different taste with Livingston's water.

## Fluoride

Livingston does not add fluoride to the water. New Jersey American Water and the East Orange Water Commission also do not add fluoride to the water. However, all water contains some naturally occurring fluoride.

## Water Main Breaks

Water main breaks can result in many of the issues outlined above. If water quality issues are associated with a water main break, please consider the additional steps listed below:

* Empty and clean your automatic ice makers and water chillers.
* Service connections with a water softener/cartridge filters should be run through a regeneration cycle or other procedures recommended by the manufacturer.
* If pressure is still low at a certain faucet, take off the aerator and clear off sediment using water or vinegar.
* Drain and refill your hot water heater if the temperature is set below 113 degrees Fahrenheit.
* Water reservoirs in tall buildings should be drained and refilled (as applicable).
